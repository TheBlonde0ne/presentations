Optical & Magneto-Optical Signatures of Altermagnetism

Welcome everyone. For our eighth seminar, we're shifting our focus from the surface-sensitive techniques like ARPES, which have beautifully mapped out the altermagnetic band structures, to the bulk. Today, we will explore how we use light—photons—as a clean, powerful probe of the unique electronic properties deep inside these fascinating materials. We will cover the fundamental theory of how altermagnetism manifests in the optical response, discuss the state-of-the-art experimental techniques required to measure it, and then dive into a critical comparison of recent results on key material candidates.

Context: From Surface to Bulk Probes

  • Previous Talks: Established the theoretical framework of altermagnetism, including Landau theory, spin-space groups, and the crucial role of non-symmorphic symmetries.
  • ARPES (Topic 4): Provided the first direct visualization of the momentum-dependent spin-splitting ($E(\mathbf{k},\uparrow) \neq E(\mathbf{k},\downarrow)$) at the material's surface.
  • My Focus: We move from the surface to the bulk. Optical spectroscopy measures the integrated response of the entire electronic band structure, making it a complementary and essential probe.
  • Central Question: How does the unique spin-splitting of altermagnets, without a net magnetization, manifest in their interaction with light?
The previous talks have done a fantastic job setting the stage. We understand the theory, and we've seen the compelling ARPES data that directly visualizes the k-dependent spin-splitting at the surface. But a crucial question remains: is this phenomenon robust in the bulk of the material? Optical and magneto-optical spectroscopy are our primary tools to answer this. Unlike ARPES, which is inherently surface-sensitive, optics probes the collective electronic excitations deep within the crystal. Our goal today is to understand the specific optical fingerprints that altermagnetism should produce and how to measure them.

Off-Diagonal Optical Conductivity

  • The interaction of light with a medium: $D = \varepsilon_0 \tilde{\varepsilon}_{ij} E$
    complex optical conductivity tensor, $\tilde{\sigma}_{ij}(\omega)$ or dielectric tensor, $\tilde{\varepsilon}_{ij}(\omega)$: .

Without Magnetization

$$\tilde{\varepsilon} = \begin{pmatrix} {\varepsilon}_{xx} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & {\varepsilon}_{xx} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & {\varepsilon}_{xx} \end{pmatrix}$$

With Magnetization

$$\tilde{\varepsilon} = \begin{pmatrix} \tilde{\varepsilon}_{xx} & \tilde{\varepsilon}_{xy} & 0 \\ -\tilde{\varepsilon}_{xy} & \tilde{\varepsilon}_{xx} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \tilde{\varepsilon}_{zz} \end{pmatrix}$$

  • Time-reversal symmetry ($\mathcal{T}$) breaking is required for non-zero off-diagonal elements ($\tilde{\sigma}_{xy} \neq 0$).
  • In ferromagnets, net magnetization $\mathbf{M}$ breaks $\mathcal{T}$, so $\tilde{\sigma}_{xy} \propto \mathbf{M}$.
  • In altermagnets, $\mathbf{M}=0$. However, the combined symmetry of $\mathcal{T}$ and a crystal rotation/translation is broken by the spin order. This is sufficient to allow $\tilde{\sigma}_{xy} \neq 0$.
Sato & Ishibashi, Front. Phys. (2022); Iguchi et al., Phys. Rev. Research (2025)
The fundamental signature we are looking for lies within the optical conductivity tensor. In any material, breaking time-reversal symmetry allows for the existence of off-diagonal components, like sigma_xy. In a standard ferromagnet, this is easy to understand: the net magnetization M breaks time-reversal, and the anomalous Hall conductivity sigma_xy is directly proportional to M. Altermagnets present a more subtle case. While their net magnetization is zero, the specific spin ordering breaks a combined symmetry—time-reversal followed by a crystal symmetry operation. This is enough to generate a non-zero sigma_xy. This off-diagonal conductivity is the "smoking gun" we're after. It manifests as the Magneto-Optical Kerr Effect, where the real (dissipative) part drives the Kerr ellipticity, and the imaginary (dispersive) part drives the Kerr rotation. Measuring these two angles gives us direct access to the complex sigma_xy.

The Magneto-Optical Kerr Effect

The complex Kerr effect directly probes this tensor:
$$\theta_K + i\eta_K \propto \tilde{\sigma}_{xy}(\omega)$$

  • $\theta_K$ (Rotation) $\leftrightarrow$ Dispersive part, Im($\tilde{\sigma}_{xy}$)
  • $\eta_K$ (Ellipticity) $\leftrightarrow$ Dissipative part, Re($\tilde{\sigma}_{xy}$)

Polarization Conventions:

  • a: polar
  • b: longitudinal
  • c: transversal

Kramers-Kronig Relations

Concept: Relate the real and imaginary parts of a response function.
Condition: Must be a response function (analytic in the upper half-plane).
Application: In MOKKA, we treat $f(\omega)$ as a response function, allowing us to extract the dichroism ratio from the Kerr angle.

$$ \text{Re}[f(\omega)] = \frac{1}{\pi} \mathcal{P} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{\text{Im}[f(x)]}{x - \omega} dx \hspace{2em} \text{Im}[f(\omega)] = -\frac{1}{\pi} \mathcal{P} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{\text{Re}[f(x)]}{x - \omega} dx $$

https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/45/Kramersgifwiki.gif: 03.05.2026

Magneto-Optical Kramers-Kronig Analysis

  1. Measurement: Use linear polarizers to get total reflectivity $R(\omega)$ and Kerr rotation $\theta_K(\omega)$.
  2. Define Auxiliary Function: $f(\omega) = \ln\left(\frac{\tilde{r}_-}{\tilde{r}_+}\right) = \ln c(\omega) + 2i\theta_K(\omega)$
  3. Apply Kramers-Kronig: Since $f(\omega)$ is causal, we can calculate the real part (dichroism ratio $c(\omega)$) from the measured imaginary part ($\theta_K(\omega)$). $$ \ln c(\omega) = \frac{2\omega}{\pi} \mathcal{P} \int_0^\infty \frac{2\theta_K(x)}{x^2 - \omega^2} dx $$
  4. Reconstruct & Solve: With $\left[R = (R_+ + R_-)/2\right]$ and $\left[c = R_-/R_+\right]$, we solve for $R_+$ and $R_-$ individually. A final KK transform on these yields the full complex $\tilde{r}_\pm(\omega)$.

This method avoids the spectral limitations of wave plates and is highly sensitive to small signals, making it ideal for altermagnets.

Levallois et al., Rev. Sci. Instrum. 86, 033906 (2015)
Measuring the tiny MO signals in altermagnets requires extreme precision. Standard techniques like ellipsometry, which use quarter-wave plates, are often plagued by chromatic aberrations and are difficult to implement over a broad energy range. MOKKA is the superior solution. It's a brilliant experimental and analytical protocol. The core idea is to leverage the fundamental principle of causality, embodied by the Kramers-Kronig relations. We start by measuring two simple quantities with linear polarizers: the total reflectivity and the Kerr rotation angle. The mathematical trick is to recognize that the logarithm of the ratio of the left- and right-handed complex reflectivities is itself a causal response function. We have measured its imaginary part—the Kerr angle. Therefore, we can use the Kramers-Kronig integral to calculate its real part, which is the logarithm of the circular dichroism ratio. Once we have both the sum and the ratio of the reflectivities, we can algebraically separate them. A final KK transform gives us the phases, and we have the complete optical response without ever touching a wave plate.

MOKKA: The 45-Degree Fast Protocol

For experiments in extreme conditions (high B-field, low T), rotating optics is impossible. MOKKA enables a static, "fast protocol".

  • Setup: Polarizer and analyzer are fixed at 45° to each other. This geometry maximizes sensitivity to rotation.
  • Intensity Signal: The detector intensity becomes directly proportional to the Kerr angle. For small angles: $$ I_{\pm45^\circ} \approx C \cdot R(\omega) \frac{1 \pm 2\theta_K(\omega)}{2} $$
  • Extraction: By measuring the intensity ratio at positive and negative magnetic fields, we can isolate $\theta_K$: $$ \rho_{\pm45^\circ}(B) = \frac{I_{\pm45^\circ}(+B)}{I_{\pm45^\circ}(-B)} \approx 1 \pm 4\theta_K(B) $$
Levallois et al., Rev. Sci. Instrum. 86, 033906 (2015)
One of the most powerful applications of the MOKKA framework is the fast measurement protocol. In many experiments, especially those at cryogenic temperatures and inside high-field magnets, we simply cannot have moving parts. Rotating a polarizer is not an option. The fast protocol solves this. By fixing the polarizer and analyzer at 45 degrees to each other, we place the system at the point of maximum sensitivity to any small rotation of the polarization plane. The intensity measured by the detector becomes a simple linear function of the Kerr angle. By flipping the magnetic field and taking a ratio of the intensities, all common mode factors like the sample reflectivity and instrument throughput cancel out, leaving us with a signal directly proportional to the Kerr rotation. This is a game-changer, allowing us to perform these sensitive measurements in the most challenging experimental environments.

MOKKA: The 45-Degree Fast Protocol

This allows for reference-free, in-situ measurement of the Kerr rotation, a critical capability for studying field-induced phenomena in altermagnets.

Success in Organic Altermagnet $\kappa$-Cl

  • Material: $\kappa$-(BEDT-TTF)₂Cu[N(CN)₂]Cl, a quasi-2D organic Mott insulator.
  • Key Signature: A highly non-linear dependence of the MOKE signal on the applied magnetic field.
  • The signal does not track the small canted moment (linear in H), but instead scales with the Néel vector, the primary antiferromagnetic order parameter.
  • The off-diagonal conductivity $\tilde{\sigma}_{ca}(\omega)$ shows distinct peaks at the edges of the $\pi$-electron interband transition.

Fig: Non-linear field dependence of Kerr rotation in $\kappa$-Cl. The signal emerges sharply around 6-7 T, inconsistent with a simple canted moment.

Iguchi et al., Phys. Rev. Research 7, 033026 (2025)
Now let's look at a success story. The organic material kappa-Cl is a fantastic candidate because its constituent elements are light, meaning its intrinsic spin-orbit coupling is extremely weak. Any large MO effect cannot come from conventional mechanisms. The key finding from Iguchi and colleagues is the non-linear field dependence of the MOKE signal. In a simple canted antiferromagnet, the signal would be linear with the applied field. Here, it's nearly zero until a critical field of about 6 Tesla, where it turns on sharply. This behavior is a hallmark of a response coupled not to the net moment, but to the underlying antiferromagnetic Néel vector. Furthermore, when they analyzed the frequency dependence, they found that the off-diagonal conductivity spectrum wasn't flat; it had clear peaks corresponding to the energy scale of the electronic transitions within the material's pi-electron bands.

The Decisive Argument for $\kappa$-Cl: Energy Scales

How do we prove the signal is from Altermagnetism and not just Spin-Orbit Coupling (SOC)?
  • SOC Energy Scale ($\lambda_{SOC}$): SOC is a relativistic effect. In the light organic molecules of $\kappa$-Cl, its energy scale is minuscule: $$ \lambda_{SOC} \approx 1 \text{ meV} $$
  • Observed Splitting ($E_{splitting}$): The energy separation of the peaks in $\tilde{\sigma}_{ca}(\omega)$ provides a direct measure of the spin-splitting of the electronic bands. This was found to be: $$ E_{splitting} \approx 100 \text{ meV} $$
Iguchi et al., Phys. Rev. Research 7, 033026 (2025)
The most powerful piece of evidence for altermagnetism in kappa-Cl comes from a simple comparison of energy scales. Spin-orbit coupling is a relativistic effect, and its strength is determined by the atomic number of the elements involved. For the light carbon, hydrogen, and nitrogen atoms in this organic material, the SOC energy scale is tiny, on the order of a single millielectronvolt. However, the energy splitting observed in the magneto-optical spectrum—which directly reflects the splitting of the electronic bands—is around 100 meV. This is a hundred-fold discrepancy. There is no way that a 1 meV interaction can produce a 100 meV splitting. This definitively rules out SOC as the primary origin. The splitting must come from a non-relativistic mechanism, which in this context is the momentum-dependent crystal field effect that defines altermagnetism.

Case Study 2: The RuO₂ Controversy

RuO₂ was a canonical theoretical candidate for altermagnetism. However, optical spectroscopy on high-quality bulk crystals tells a different story.

Finding 1: The Plasma Frequency

  • The low-energy (intraband) response was used to extract the plasma frequency, $\omega_p$.
  • $\omega_p$ depends on carrier density and effective mass ($m^*$).
  • Result: The experimental $\omega_p$ perfectly matched DFT calculations for a non-magnetic metal.
  • The altermagnetic model, which requires correlations that increase $m^*$, predicted a much lower $\omega_p$, contradicting the data.
Wenzel et al., Phys. Rev. B 111, L041115 (2025)
The case of Ruthenium Dioxide is a crucial lesson in scientific rigor. It was predicted by many theories to be a model altermagnet. However, when Wenzel and colleagues performed careful broadband infrared spectroscopy on high-quality bulk single crystals, their findings contradicted the magnetic theory. They found two "killer" pieces of evidence. First, they analyzed the Drude response of the free carriers to determine the plasma frequency. This value was in excellent agreement with calculations for a simple, non-magnetic metal. The altermagnetic theory, which requires electronic correlations, would have resulted in a significantly different value. Second, and perhaps more decisively, they observed a sharp "Pauli edge" in the interband absorption spectrum. This feature is a direct fingerprint of a specific topological feature—a Dirac nodal line—that exists only in the non-magnetic band structure of RuO2. The altermagnetic state would eliminate this feature. Seeing it so clearly in the experiment is strong evidence that the bulk ground state is, in fact, non-magnetic.

Fermi-Liquid Behavior in RuO₂

The intraband carrier dynamics provided the final piece of the puzzle. The data was analyzed using an Extended Drude Model to account for electron-electron scattering.

  • The model assumes a scattering rate that depends on temperature and frequency: $$ 1/\tau(\omega, T) = 1/\tau_0 + A \cdot [(\hbar\omega)^2 + (p\pi k_B T)^2] $$
  • This quadratic dependence is the textbook signature of a Fermi Liquid, a well-behaved paramagnetic metal.
  • The experimental data for RuO₂ fit this model perfectly, confirming its non-magnetic, metallic ground state.
Conclusion: While altermagnetism may be present in strained thin films of RuO₂, the ground state of the bulk crystal is a non-magnetic Fermi liquid. This highlights the importance of bulk-sensitive probes and high-quality samples.
Wenzel et al., Phys. Rev. B 111, L041115 (2025)
To put the final nail in the coffin for the bulk altermagnetism theory in RuO2, the authors analyzed the dynamics of the free carriers themselves. In a simple metal, carriers scatter off impurities at a constant rate. But in a correlated metal, they also scatter off each other, and this process has a very specific quadratic dependence on both temperature and frequency. This is the hallmark of what we call a Fermi liquid. By using an Extended Drude Model that incorporates this behavior, they found a perfect fit to their experimental data. This confirmed that the electrons in bulk RuO2 behave exactly as expected for a standard paramagnetic metal. The takeaway is crucial: claims of altermagnetism must be carefully checked against alternative, non-magnetic explanations, and sample quality—especially the distinction between thin films and bulk crystals—is paramount.

Indirect Probes: Phonon Circular Dichroism

Even if the electronic MO signal is weak, magnetism can be detected indirectly through its coupling to the lattice, as shown in the Weyl ferromagnet Co₃Sn₂S₂.

  • Observation: Infrared-active phonons show Circular Dichroism (CD), meaning they absorb left- and right-circularly polarized light differently.
  • Mechanism: This is not an intrinsic phonon property. It arises from Fano Resonance, an interference between the discrete phonon and the continuous electronic excitations of the chiral Weyl fermions.

Yellow = Resultant Elliptical Path (CD Effect)

Yang et al., Phys. Rev. Lett. 134, 196905 (2025)
So what if the direct electronic MO signal is ambiguous? We can look for indirect fingerprints. A beautiful example comes from the study of the Weyl ferromagnet Co3Sn2S2. Here, the authors found that the phonons—the lattice vibrations—exhibited circular dichroism. This is a remarkable finding, as phonons are typically not chiral. The origin is a Fano resonance. This occurs when a discrete excitation, like a phonon, can interfere with a continuum of excitations, in this case, the chiral electronic states near the Weyl points. This interference leads to a characteristic asymmetric lineshape. The degree of asymmetry, given by the Fano parameter q, is a direct measure of the electron-phonon coupling strength. Crucially, this effect only appeared below the magnetic ordering temperature. This proves that the phonons are coupling to the magnetic and chiral electronic system, effectively "borrowing" its circular dichroism. This provides a powerful, indirect method to confirm the presence of a chiral magnetic state, a technique directly applicable to altermagnets.

Indirect Probes: Phonon Circular Dichroism

Even if the electronic MO signal is weak, magnetism can be detected indirectly through its coupling to the lattice, as shown in the Weyl ferromagnet Co₃Sn₂S₂.

  • The lineshape asymmetry, quantified by the Fano parameter $q$, measures the electron-phonon coupling strength: $$ \sigma(\omega) \propto \frac{(q + \epsilon)^2}{1 + \epsilon^2} \quad \text{where} \quad \epsilon = \frac{\omega - \omega_0}{\gamma} $$
  • The phonon CD and Fano asymmetry appear only below the Curie temperature, proving the phonons are "borrowing" chirality from the magnetic electronic system. This is a powerful indirect proof of magnetism.

Summary & Outlook

Optical spectroscopy provides a powerful, bulk-sensitive toolkit for identifying and characterizing altermagnets.

Material Key Optical Signature Altermagnetic Evidence Strength
$\kappa$-Cl Non-linear MOKE; $E_{splitting} \gg \lambda_{SOC}$ Very Strong
RuO₂ (Bulk) Pauli Edge & Plasma Frequency match non-magnetic model Strong Negative
Co₃Sn₂S₂ Phonon Circular Dichroism via Fano Resonance Strong (Indirect Proof of Principle)
To bring everything together, we've seen that optical spectroscopy is a crucial tool in the altermagnetism toolkit. It provides a bulk-sensitive probe that is complementary to surface techniques like ARPES. We've established that the off-diagonal optical conductivity is the key fingerprint we're looking for, and that advanced techniques like MOKKA are required to measure it accurately. The tale of kappa-Cl and RuO2 serves as a perfect illustration of both the power of this approach and the need for scientific rigor. The non-linear field dependence and, most importantly, the massive energy scale mismatch in kappa-Cl provide compelling evidence for altermagnetism. In contrast, the clear signatures of a non-magnetic Fermi liquid in bulk RuO2 show that we must always test against alternative hypotheses. The future lies in applying these rigorous optical methods to the growing family of altermagnet candidates. Thank you for your attention, and I'd be happy to take any questions.
