How do we identify Altermagnets?

Among some of the predictions, many have been later contested. For example: early ARPES studies on RuO2 claimed to see the characteristic spin-splitting, but later work showed that the observed features could be explained by surface effects or other non-altermagnetic mechanisms. Today i will talk about how we can use optical and magneto-optical techniques to probe the bulk properties of altermagnets, providing a more definitive fingerprint.

Before we dive into the experimental techniques, let's first understand the theoretical framework that connects the unique band structure of altermagnets to their optical properties. The condition for altermagnetism is a net magnetization of zero, but a special symmetry which leads to the breaking of time reversal symmetry and preservation of time reversal symmetry in combination with a spatial transformation. This leads to a unique spin-splitting in the band structure, which should manifest in the optical response of the material. P is inversion T is timereversal t is translation

b: Anti-Ferro

The necessary Symmetry Condition

Now that we understand the symmetry conditions that define altermagnetism, we can explore how this unique band structure leads to distinctive optical properties. The key quantity we will focus on is the optical conductivity tensor, which describes how the material responds to an applied electric field at different frequencies. In particular, we will see how the off-diagonal elements of this tensor can serve as a fingerprint for altermagnetism, distinguishing it from both ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic materials. If no magnetization is present, the conductivity tensor is diagonal. In a ferromagnet, the magnetization leads to off-diagonal elements due to the breaking of time-reversal symmetry. In an altermagnet, we also get off-diagonal elements, but they arise from the unique symmetry of the spin-splitting in k-space, even though the net magnetization is zero. This is a key point that we will explore in detail.

Now that we understand the theoretical basis for the magneto-optical response in altermagnets, we can discuss how to experimentally measure this response. The primary technique used to probe the off-diagonal elements of the optical conductivity tensor is the Magneto-Optical Kerr Effect, or MOKE. In a MOKE experiment, we shine polarized light onto the surface of a magnetized material and measure how the polarization state of the reflected light changes. The Kerr rotation angle \(\theta_K\) and ellipticity \(\eta_K\) are directly related to the real and imaginary parts of the off-diagonal conductivity \(\tilde{\sigma}_{xy}\). By carefully analyzing these changes in polarization, we can extract information about the optical conductivity tensor and thus gain insights into the underlying electronic structure of the altermagnet. This technique is particularly powerful because it allows us to probe the bulk properties of the material, providing a complementary perspective to surface-sensitive techniques like ARPES.

$$\tilde{\varepsilon} = \begin{pmatrix} \color{#8b4e5d}{\tilde{\varepsilon}_{xx}} & \color{#387066}{\tilde{\varepsilon}_{xy}} \\ -\color{#387066}{\tilde{\varepsilon}_{xy}} & \color{#8b4e5d}{\tilde{\varepsilon}_{xx}} \end{pmatrix} \rightarrow \tilde{\varepsilon}_{\pm} = \color{#8b4e5d}{\tilde{\varepsilon}_{xx}} \pm i\color{#387066}{\tilde{\varepsilon}_{xy}}$$
  • Absolute Reflectivity \(R(\omega)\)
  • Phase \(\theta(\omega)\)

Complex reflectivity: $$r(\omega) = \sqrt{R(\omega)} e^{i\theta(\omega)} = \frac{1- \sqrt{\tilde{\varepsilon}_{\pm}(\omega)}}{1+\sqrt{\tilde{\varepsilon}_{\pm}(\omega)}}$$

The function \(f(\omega) = \ln r(\omega)\) is a response function:
analytic in the upper half-plane.

What i havent mentioned yet is that the measurement of MOKE only results in information about the imaginary/real part of the off diagonal elements. Fortunately, the optical response of a material follows a response function, which is analytic in the upper half-plane. A handy tool we all heard of in exphys 6 is the kramers-kronig relation. This means that one part of the complex tensor element is enough to calculate the remaining part by integrating in fourier space. This is a common relation between the imaginary and real part of the refractive index and is transferable to the optical conductivity tensor.

Kramers-Kronig Relations

Connecting Real and Imaginary Parts

Condition: Response function (analytic in the upper half-plane).
$$ \color{#8f5060}{\text{Re}[f(\omega)]} = \frac{1}{\pi} \mathcal{P} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{\color{#428377}{\text{Im}[f(x)]}}{x - \omega} dx$$
$$ \color{#428377}{\text{Im}[f(\omega)]} = -\frac{1}{\pi} \mathcal{P} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{\color{#8f5060}{\text{Re}[f(x)]}}{x - \omega} dx $$

https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/45/Kramersgifwiki.gif: 03.05.2026

Magneto-Optical Kramers-Kronig Analysis

  1. Measurement: Use linear polarizers to get total reflectivity $R(\omega)$ and Kerr rotation $\theta_K(\omega)$.
  2. Define Auxiliary Function: $$f(\omega) = \ln\left(\frac{\tilde{r}_-}{\tilde{r}_+}\right) = \color{#8f5060}{\ln c(\omega)} + i \cdot \color{#428377}{2\theta_K(\omega)}$$
  3. Apply Kramers-Kronig: $$ \color{#8f5060}{\ln c(\omega)} = \frac{2\omega}{\pi} \mathcal{P} \int_0^\infty \frac{\color{#428377}{2\theta_K(x)}}{x^2 - \omega^2} dx $$
  4. Reconstruct & Solve: With $\left[R = (R_+ + R_-)/2\right]$ and $\left[c = R_-/R_+\right]$, we solve for $R_+$ and $R_-$ individually. A final KK transform on these yields the full complex $\tilde{r}_\pm(\omega)$.

This method avoids the spectral limitations of wave plates and is highly sensitive to small signals, making it ideal for altermagnets.

Levallois et al., Rev. Sci. Instrum. 86, 033906 (2015)
Measuring the tiny MO signals in altermagnets requires extreme precision. Standard techniques like ellipsometry, which use quarter-wave plates, are often plagued by chromatic aberrations and are difficult to implement over a broad energy range. MOKKA is the superior solution. It's a brilliant experimental and analytical protocol. The core idea is to leverage the fundamental principle of causality, embodied by the Kramers-Kronig relations. We start by measuring two simple quantities with linear polarizers: the total reflectivity and the Kerr rotation angle. The mathematical trick is to recognize that the logarithm of the ratio of the left- and right-handed complex reflectivities is itself a causal response function. We have measured its imaginary part—the Kerr angle. Therefore, we can use the Kramers-Kronig integral to calculate its real part, which is the logarithm of the circular dichroism ratio. Once we have both the sum and the ratio of the reflectivities, we can algebraically separate them. A final KK transform gives us the phases, and we have the complete optical response without ever touching a wave plate.

MOKKA: The 45-Degree Fast Protocol

Extreme conditions (high B-field, low T): rotating optics is not possible

  • Setup: Polarizer and analyzer are fixed at 45° to each other. This geometry maximizes sensitivity to rotation.
  • Intensity Signal: The detector intensity becomes directly proportional to the Kerr angle. For small angles: $$ I_{\pm45^\circ} \approx C \cdot R(\omega) \frac{1 \pm 2\theta_K(\omega)}{2} $$
  • Extraction: Measure intensity ratio at positive and negative magnetic fields and isolate $\theta_K$: $$ \rho_{\pm45^\circ}(B) = \frac{I_{\pm45^\circ}(+B)}{I_{\pm45^\circ}(-B)} \approx 1 \pm 4\theta_K(B) $$
Levallois et al., Rev. Sci. Instrum. 86, 033906 (2015)
One of the most powerful applications of the MOKKA framework is the fast measurement protocol. In many experiments, especially those at cryogenic temperatures and inside high-field magnets, we simply cannot have moving parts. Rotating a polarizer is not an option. The fast protocol solves this. By fixing the polarizer and analyzer at 45 degrees to each other, we place the system at the point of maximum sensitivity to any small rotation of the polarization plane. The intensity measured by the detector becomes a simple linear function of the Kerr angle. By flipping the magnetic field and taking a ratio of the intensities, all common mode factors like the sample reflectivity and instrument throughput cancel out, leaving us with a signal directly proportional to the Kerr rotation. This is a game-changer, allowing us to perform these sensitive measurements in the most challenging experimental environments.

We need a path from band stucture to optical response:
Kubo Fomula

Now that we have a solid understanding of the theoretical framework and the experimental techniques, we can dive into the mathematical details of how the optical conductivity tensor is calculated from the electronic band structure. The key equation that describes this relationship is the Kubo formula, which expresses the optical conductivity as a sum over all possible electronic transitions between occupied and unoccupied states in the Brillouin zone. The Kubo formula includes a resonance condition in the denominator, which dictates when a peak in the optical response occurs, and matrix elements that relate to the Berry curvature of the bands. In altermagnets, due to their unique symmetry properties, these matrix elements do not cancel out when summed over the Brillouin zone, leading to a net non-zero off-diagonal conductivity \(\tilde{\sigma}_{xy}\). This is a crucial point that allows us to connect the theoretical predictions with experimental observations and provides a powerful tool for identifying altermagnetism in materials. Another reason we need a quantum mechanical description is that we can simulate the optical conductivity using DFT calculations. By calculating the band structure and the relevant matrix elements, we can predict the optical response of a material and compare it directly with experimental measurements, providing a stringent test for the presence of altermagnetism. But DFT simulations still need an order to be initialized. So for comparison we run simulations for every magnetic order type and compare the resulting optical response with the measurements to determine the correct order. In a $\mathcal{PT}$-symmetric system, the Berry curvature at $\mathbf{k}$ is exactly cancelled by the Berry curvature at $-\mathbf{k}$, leading to $\sigma_{xy} = 0$. Altermagnets break $\mathcal{PT}$, allowing this 'topological' property to survive the integration over the Brillouin zone.

The Kubo Formula

Linking Band Structure to Optical Response

\[\tilde{\sigma}_{xy}(\omega) \propto \frac{1}{\omega} \sum_{n, m} \int_{\text{BZ}} d\text{k} \frac{f(E_{n, \text{{k}}}) - f(E_{m, \text{k}})}{{\color{#428377}{E_{m, \text{k}} - E_{n, \text{k}} - \hbar \omega}} - i\Gamma} \times \color{#8f5060}{\langle \psi_{n, \text{k}} | \hat{v}_{x} | \psi_{m, \text{k}}\rangle \langle{ \psi_{m, \text{k}} | \hat{v}_{y} | \psi_{n, \text{k}} }\rangle} \]

1. The Resonance (Denominator)

  • \((E_m - E_n - \hbar\omega)\) dictates when a peak occurs
  • Vertical interband transitions at specific \(\text{k}\)-points

2. The Topology (Matrix Elements)

  • \(\langle \hat{v}_x \rangle \langle \hat{v}_y \rangle\) relates to orbital angular momentum of the transition
  • In altermagnets, this product survives the BZ sum

◆Symmetry Note:
In standard AFMs, \(\sigma_{xy}(\text{k}) + \sigma_{xy}(\mathcal{PT}\text{k}) = 0\).

◆Altermagnetism:
Alternating spin-polarization in \(\text{k}\)-space \(\rightarrow\) net non-zero sum for the off-diagonal elements.

The Kubo Formula

\[\tilde{\sigma}_{xy}(\omega) \propto \dots \sum \int \frac{\Delta f}{\Delta E - \hbar\omega} \times \langle \hat{v}_x \rangle \langle \hat{v}_y \rangle \]

Standard AFM: \(\sigma_{xy} = 0\)
Altermagnet: \(\sigma_{xy} \neq 0\)
Essentially, the optical conductivity is a sum of all possible electronic transitions from an occupied band ($n$) to an unoccupied band ($m$). First, the denominator: when the photon energy $\hbar\omega$ matches the band energy difference, we see a resonance peak. In $FeSb_2$, this identified the $0.1 \text{ eV}$ transition. Second, the velocity matrix elements serve as selection rules. In standard AFMs with $\mathcal{PT}$ symmetry, contributions at $k$ and $\mathcal{PT}k$ cancel out. However, altermagnetic spin-polarization allows for a net non-zero sum, creating a finite $\sigma_{xy}$ without net magnetization.

evidenced by a sharp drop in electrical resistivity below 100 K and the emergence of a coherent Drude response in the optical conductivity. The strong itinerant spin fluctuations that plague the parent compound are substantially quenched. This is quantitatively supported by a dramatic reduction in the Drude scattering rate (from \(\approx 905 \text{ cm}^{-1}\) in the parent to \(\approx 559 \text{ cm}^{-1}\) in the doped system at 300 K), despite the introduction of impurity scattering from the Co dopants. Itinerant spin fluctuations act as a massive scattering channel for electrons. When Co-doping stabilizes the altermagnetic order, it quenches these chaotic fluctuations. Therefore, the electrons scatter less, and the scattering rate drops—even though you just added chemical impurities (Cobalt) to the lattice!
